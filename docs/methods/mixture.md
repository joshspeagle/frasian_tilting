# mixture

> Status: `implemented`

## Summary

The **m-geodesic** of information geometry: linear interpolation in
density space between the posterior and the likelihood-as-Gaussian,

```
q(theta; eta) = (1 - eta) · post(theta) + eta · L(theta) / Z_L,
eta in [0, 1].
```

This is the dually flat partner of `power_law`'s **e-geodesic** under
the Fisher metric (Amari 1982; Amari & Nagaoka 2000): power-law is
the log-linear / geometric-mean path, mixture is the linear-density /
arithmetic-mean path. On the Normal-Normal sandbox the output is a
two-component Gaussian mixture — *not* a Gaussian itself — and is
bimodal when prior and data disagree strongly, breaking WALDO's
unimodal-Gaussian assumptions.

## Motivation

`power_law` and `mixture` are not "main scheme" and "naive baseline"
— they are the two natural geodesics under the Fisher metric, the
**e-geodesic** (α=+1 connection) and **m-geodesic** (α=-1 connection)
of Amari's dually flat structure (Amari 1982 §4; Amari & Nagaoka 2000
Theorem 3.4). Both are smooth in their respective coordinate systems
(natural parameters for e, expectation parameters for m) and admit
canonical Pythagorean / projection theorems. The Wasserstein geodesic
(`ot`) is a third, distinct geometry (Pistone & Malagò 2018).

This scheme exists to:

1. **Realise the dual partner of `power_law`.** The framework that
   implements only the e-geodesic is geometrically lopsided; running
   the m-geodesic alongside makes the dually-flat structure
   observable in the smoothness / coverage diagnostics.
2. **Test the bimodality hypothesis.** Mixtures of Gaussians are
   bimodal when components are far apart relative to their widths
   (Behboodian 1970; Robertson & Fryer 1969). On the Normal-Normal
   sandbox the threshold for guaranteed unimodality is
   `|mu_n - D| ≤ 2 min(sigma_n, sigma)`, which translates to
   `|Delta| ≤ 2 sqrt(w)` in the framework's normalised conflict
   coordinate. Beyond that threshold the WALDO acceptance-region
   inversion may fail (no unique CI, or HPD-set semantics required).

## Definition

For two 1D distributions `p` and `q` with densities, the m-geodesic
**within the mixture family generated by `{p, q}`** is

```
q(theta; eta) = (1 - eta) · p(theta) + eta · q(theta),  eta in [0, 1]
```

(Amari & Nagaoka 2000 §2.4; Amari 2016 §2.4). The m-geodesic is
*affine in m-coordinates* (the mixture-weight parameterisation); it
is the dual to the e-geodesic, which is affine in e-coordinates
(natural parameters).

**Endpoints.** Following the framework's posterior <-> likelihood
convention (matches `power_law` and `ot`): `p = post`, `q = L /
Z_L = N(D, sigma^2)` on the Normal-Normal sandbox. So `eta = 0`
recovers the WALDO posterior (identity element) and `eta = 1`
recovers the likelihood-as-Gaussian.

**Closed-form on Normal-Normal.** With `mu_n = w*D + (1-w)*mu0`,
`sigma_n = sqrt(w)*sigma`, and `(m_1, s_1) = (mu_n, sigma_n)`,
`(m_2, s_2) = (D, sigma)`:

```
p_eta(theta) = (1 - eta) * N(theta; m_1, s_1^2) + eta * N(theta; m_2, s_2^2)
mean(p_eta)  = (1 - eta) * m_1 + eta * m_2
var(p_eta)   = (1 - eta) * s_1^2 + eta * s_2^2 + eta * (1 - eta) * (m_1 - m_2)^2
```

For Gaussian endpoints the output is a **two-component Gaussian
mixture**, not Gaussian itself; the framework's
`MixtureDistribution` (in `frasian/models/distributions.py`) wraps
this object and exposes the `Distribution` protocol surface
(pdf / logpdf / cdf / quantile / mean / var / sample / fingerprint).

## Derivation

Full step-by-step derivation lives at
`audit/tier2/mixture_derivation.md` (499 lines, MC-verified at
N=2e6 and quadrature-verified at atol < 1e-15). Summary:

**Step 1 — m-geodesic density.** The m-connection on a statistical
manifold is the affine connection whose geodesics are linear in the
*expectation* parameters (`m-coordinates`); equivalently, on a
mixture family the geodesic is the linear interpolation of
densities (Amari 1982 §3; Amari & Nagaoka 2000 Theorem 3.4). For the
mixture family `{(1-eta)p + eta·q : eta in [0, 1]}` the
m-coordinates are exactly the mixing weights, so the m-geodesic is
`(1-eta)p + eta·q`. Convex combination is automatically normalised:
`integral p_eta = (1-eta)*1 + eta*1 = 1` for every eta.

**Step 2 — Bimodality threshold.** Behboodian 1970: a 2-Gaussian
mixture is *guaranteed unimodal* iff `|m_1 - m_2| <= 2 min(s_1, s_2)`.
Substituting `(m_1, s_1) = (mu_n, sigma_n)` and `(m_2, s_2) = (D, sigma)`
with `sigma_n = sqrt(w)*sigma <= sigma`, the threshold reduces to
`|Delta| <= 2*sqrt(w)` in the framework's normalised conflict
coordinate. Numerical verification (Tier 2.1, w=0.5, eta=0.5):
empirically bimodal beyond `|Delta| ~ 2`; the Behboodian sufficient
bound at `1.414` is conservative as expected.

**Step 3 — Moments.** Sympy-verified (and quadrature-verified at
atol < 1e-15):

```
mean(p_eta) = (1 - eta) * mu_n + eta * D
var(p_eta)  = (1 - eta) * sigma_n^2 + eta * sigma^2
            + eta * (1 - eta) * (mu_n - D)^2
```

The cross-term `eta(1-eta)(mu_n - D)^2 <= (mu_n - D)^2 / 4` (max at
eta=1/2), so `var(p_eta) <= max(sigma_n^2, sigma^2) + (mu_n - D)^2 / 4`.

**Step 4a — Tilted Wald.** Wald uses the MLE `D` and the model
`sigma`; the m-geodesic changes neither. So
`p_Wald^{tilted}(theta; eta) = p_Wald^{bare}(theta) = 2(1 - Phi(|theta - D|/sigma))`.

**Step 4b — Tilted WALDO.** Re-use bare WALDO's
`Phi(b - a) + Phi(-a - b)` with the *mixture's* first two moments
substituted for the posterior centre/scale. Specifically, with
`mu_eta` and `sigma_eta^2` the mixture mean/variance from Step 3,

```
a(theta; eta) = sigma * |mu_eta - theta| / sigma_eta^2
b(theta)      = (1 - w) * (mu_0 - theta) / (w * sigma)
p(theta; eta) = Phi(b - a) + Phi(-a - b)
```

This is the canonical "tilted WALDO" structure shared with
`power_law` and `fisher_rao`: bare WALDO's formula evaluated with
the tilted-distribution-aware `(mu_eta, sigma_eta)` plugged into
`a`, and `b` left as the bare prior z-score (the prior is fixed,
the m-geodesic only re-parametrises the posterior side, so `b` is
eta-independent). At `eta = 0` the mixture's component-1 weight is
1 so `mu_eta = mu_n` and `sigma_eta^2 = sigma_n^2 = w * sigma^2`,
which makes `a = sigma * |mu_n - theta| / (w * sigma^2) = |mu_n -
theta| / (w * sigma)` — bare WALDO's `a`. The formula collapses
exactly to bare WALDO at `eta = 0`, satisfying the tilting protocol's
identity invariant. Pinned by
`tests/properties/test_mixture_invariants.py::test_tilted_waldo_at_eta_zero_equals_bare_waldo`.

**Step 5 — Dynamic-eta inversion.** No closed form for `eta*` under
the mixture (variance is quadratic in eta, not rational-of-eta).
Numerical scan via `_dynamic.dynamic_ci_scan` — only the
`tilted_pvalue_fn` closure changes versus `power_law` / `ot`.

**Step 6 — Admissible range.** `eta in [0, 1]` always; convex
combination of two valid densities is itself a valid density.
Qualitatively *simpler* than `power_law`'s admissible range, which
depends on `w` through `denom = 1 - eta(1 - w)`.

**Dually-flat connection.** The dually-flat duality with `power_law`
is via the Legendre transform between e-coordinates (natural
parameters; log-linear interpolation = e-geodesic) and
m-coordinates (expectation parameters; linear-density interpolation
= m-geodesic). On a conjugate exponential family these two
coordinate systems are related by the cumulant function; under the
Fisher metric, e- and m-geodesics are mutually orthogonal in the
Pythagorean sense (Amari 1982 Theorem 1; Amari & Nagaoka 2000
Theorem 3.8). This is the natural-coordinate framing of Bayesian
**tempering** (`q^t = q^t / Z(t)` with `q = post^(1-t) · L^t`) as
the e-geodesic; the corresponding m-geodesic is exactly the linear
mixture above. Neal (2001) introduces the geometric (e-geodesic)
path in annealed importance sampling and explicitly contrasts it
with the linear/m-geodesic path; the latter is what this scheme
implements.

## Predicted behavior

- **Smooth `eta*(|Delta|)` curve.** No clamp; the path is linear in
  density space, so `tilt(...)` is continuous in η on `[0, 1]`.
- **Bimodality threshold.** Behboodian 1970: the mixture
  `(1-η)·N(μ_n, σ_n^2) + η·N(D, σ^2)` is **guaranteed unimodal**
  when `|μ_n - D| ≤ 2 · min(σ_n, σ)`. Since `σ_n = √w·σ ≤ σ`, this
  reduces to `|μ_n - D| ≤ 2√w·σ`, equivalently
  `|Δ| := (1-w)·|μ_0 - D|/σ ≤ 2√w`. In the conflict band beyond
  that, the mixture is bimodal for some η values.
- **WALDO p-value undefined under bimodality.** WALDO assumes a
  unimodal Gaussian posterior; against a bimodal mixture the
  acceptance-region inversion may not produce a single CI. The cell
  evaluator must record NaN or fall back to HPD-set semantics
  (Hyndman 1996; Box & Tiao 1973 §2.7-2.8).
- **Worse coverage than the geometric schemes** in the bimodal regime
  is expected (the test statistic was designed against Gaussians).

## Failure modes

- **Bimodality breaks WALDO acceptance-region inversion.** No single
  CI; the HPD set is the right object but the framework's
  `confidence_interval` returns a single `(lo, hi)`. Implementation
  should return the convex hull or NaN, with a flag in metadata.
- **Static-selector multi-region scan not implemented.** Phase 6
  skeptic vector #9: `MixtureTilting.confidence_regions` with a
  static `FixedEtaSelector` at high `|Delta|` (specifically when
  `|Delta| > 2 * sqrt(w)`, the Behboodian threshold) routes through
  `tilted_confidence_interval`, which calls `brentq_with_doubling`
  *once* per side and so always returns a single `(lo, hi)` tuple.
  In the bimodal regime the WALDO p-curve crosses `alpha` more than
  twice, so the static path may miss disjoint accept-region
  components and report only the convex hull around the closer
  crossing. The dynamic-selector path (`DynamicNumericalEtaSelector`
  / `LearnedDynamicEtaSelector`) routes through `dynamic_ci_scan`,
  which already enumerates all crossings and returns each disjoint
  region. **Recommendation:** for `|Delta| > 2 * sqrt(w)`, use a
  dynamic selector; static-selector results in this regime should
  be treated as the convex hull of the true accept region rather
  than the union.
- **Mixture is not in any tractable exponential family.** Closed-form
  moments + cdf only because both components are Gaussian; for
  arbitrary endpoints, numerical quadrature on the components is
  required. Same plumbing as `QuantileMixturePath` (different
  numerical concerns).
- **`Posterior` protocol assumes single-mode `quantile`.** The
  protocol does not currently distinguish; adapting to bimodal
  posteriors may require an extension.

## Invariants

- `tilt(eta=0)` returns the input posterior exactly.
- `tilt(eta=1)` returns `N(D, sigma^2)` (likelihood-as-Gaussian).
- `tilt` is continuous in `eta` (linear in density space).
- For every `eta in [0, 1]`, the output integrates to 1.
- Wald-statistic CI is well-defined (Wald ignores prior); WALDO CI
  may be undefined when the mixture is bimodal — record NaN /
  metadata flag rather than crash.

## Literature

### Foundational (information geometry, e-/m-duality)

- Amari, S. "Differential geometry of curved exponential families —
  curvatures and information loss." *Ann. Statist.* 10 (1982):
  357–385. — Introduces the dual ±1 (e and m) affine connections.
- Amari, S., Nagaoka, H. *Methods of Information Geometry.* AMS /
  Oxford, 2000. — Standard textbook; §2.4 (mixture family) and
  §3 (dually flat manifolds).
- Amari, S. *Information Geometry and Its Applications.* Springer,
  2016. — Modern textbook; §2.4 explicitly states the m-geodesic in
  the mixture family is `(1-t)p + tq`.
- Chentsov, N. N. *Statistical Decision Rules and Optimal Inference.*
  AMS, 1982 (Russian original 1972). — Uniqueness theorem for the
  Fisher metric and α-connections under invariance.
- Csiszár, I. "I-divergence geometry of probability distributions and
  minimization problems." *Ann. Probab.* 3 (1975): 146–158. — I-/m-
  projection picture predating Amari's α-formalism.
- Nielsen, F. "An elementary introduction to information geometry."
  *Entropy* 22 (2020): 1100. — Modern survey explicitly stating
  exp- and mixture-families are the canonical dually-flat pair.

### Closely related (e-geodesic / power-law dual)

- Neal, R. M. "Annealed importance sampling." *Stat. Comput.* 11
  (2001): 125–139. — Geometric path `f_0^(1-β) · f_n^β` is the
  e-geodesic; explicitly contrasted with the linear/m-geodesic.
- Friel, N., Pettitt, A. N. "Marginal likelihood estimation via power
  posteriors." *J. R. Stat. Soc. B* 70 (2008): 589–607.
- Gelman, A., Meng, X.-L. "Simulating normalizing constants: from
  importance sampling to bridge sampling to path sampling." *Stat.
  Sci.* 13 (1998): 163–185. — Connects optimal paths to
  Jeffreys/Fisher–Rao geometry.

### Closely related (mixture-of-Gaussians topology)

- Behboodian, J. "On the modes of a mixture of two normal
  distributions." *Technometrics* 12 (1970): 131–139. — **Cite for
  the unimodality threshold** `|μ_1 − μ_2| ≤ 2 min(σ_1, σ_2)`.
- Robertson, C. A., Fryer, J. G. "Some descriptive properties of
  normal mixtures." *Skand. Aktuarie.* (1969): 137–146. — Earlier
  necessary-condition result.
- Ray, S., Lindsay, B. G. "The topography of multivariate normal
  mixtures." *Ann. Statist.* 33 (2005): 2042–2065. — Multivariate
  generalisation; ridgeline manifold for mode counting.
- Hyndman, R. J. "Computing and graphing highest density regions."
  *Amer. Statist.* 50 (1996): 120–126. — HPD-set construction for
  multimodal densities.
- Box, G. E. P., Tiao, G. C. *Bayesian Inference in Statistical
  Analysis.* Wiley, 1973. — §2.7–2.8 develop HPD regions including
  the multimodal case.

### Contrasting (third geometry: optimal transport)

- Olkin, I., Pukelsheim, F. "The distance between two random vectors
  with given dispersion matrices." *Lin. Alg. Appl.* 48 (1982):
  257–263. — W2 distance between Gaussians.
- Villani, C. *Optimal Transport: Old and New.* Springer, 2008. —
  Ch. 7 (displacement interpolation) is the canonical contrast: W2
  geodesics ≠ e-geodesics ≠ m-geodesics.
- Pistone, G., Malagò, L. "Wasserstein Riemannian geometry of
  Gaussian densities." *Information Geometry* 1 (2018): 137–179. —
  Direct contrast of W2 and Amari geometries on Gaussians.

m-geodesic counterpart to `power_law`'s Dynamic-WALDO. Coverage is
